History of Computer

Charles Babbage

Considered the "father of computing," Babbage conceived the Analytical Engine in 1837. Though never fully built during his lifetime, its design incorporated key elements of modern computers, including an arithmetic logic unit (ALU), basic flow control, and integrated memory. His work laid the foundational concepts for programmable, general-purpose computers.

Konrad Zuse

Often credited as the "father of the computer," Zuse built the Z1, Z2, Z3, and Z4 computers between 1936 and 1950. The Z3, completed in 1941, is considered the first working electromechanical, program-controlled computer. His work demonstrated the practical feasibility of programmable computers.

Henry Roberts

Credited as the "father of the personal computer," Roberts released the Altair 8800 in 1974. This kit-based computer, featured on the cover of Popular Electronics magazine, sparked the personal computer revolution, making computing accessible to a wider audience.
